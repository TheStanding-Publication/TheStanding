In April 2026 the Department of Justice obtained a federal grand jury subpoena
demanding that the Fulton County, Georgia Board of Registration and Elections
turn over the names, positions, residential and email addresses, and personal
telephone numbers of essentially every person who helped administer the 2020
general election — from county employees and poll workers to volunteers and
bus drivers who operated mobile voting locations. The subpoena, dated April 17,
2026 and served on the county's elections director three days later, was
initiated by Dan Bishop, a Trump ally serving as U.S. Attorney for the Middle
District of North Carolina, and purportedly approved by a grand jury in
northern Georgia. The demand reaches back five years to an election that was
already audited, recounted, and certified.

Fulton County moved to quash the subpoena, arguing in a 27-page motion that it
was designed "to target, harass, and punish the President's perceived political
opponents" and would expose rank-and-file election workers to identification
and contact while deterring people from staffing future elections. County
officials, including Board of Commissioners chairman Robb Pitts, characterized
the demand as federal overreach and an abuse of the criminal process intended
to chill participation in elections. At a May 19, 2026 hearing the presiding
judge pressed prosecutors on how the personal information would be used; a DOJ
prosecutor confirmed the department intended to locate and interview the workers
about the 2020 ballot-processing.

The action fits The Standing's election-worker-intimidation pattern — the
compelled disclosure of home addresses and personal phone numbers exposes
ordinary election administrators to identification and contact — and its
politicized-investigations and weaponizing-DOJ markers, given the county's
framing of the demand as retaliation against perceived political opponents and
the use of grand jury process to revisit a long-settled election. The motion to
quash remained pending before the district court as of late May 2026; this
entry may warrant review once the court rules.
