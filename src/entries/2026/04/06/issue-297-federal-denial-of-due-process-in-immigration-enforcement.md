For roughly five months, a 3-year-old girl remained in U.S. federal custody after she crossed the border near El Paso with her mother on Sept. 16, 2025. When the mother was charged with making false statements and separated from her daughter, U.S. Customs and Border Protection designated the toddler an unaccompanied minor and transferred her to the Office of Refugee Resettlement, which placed her in foster care in Harlingen, Texas. Her father — a lawful permanent resident of more than ten years — submitted a sponsor application within a day of learning she was detained and, by his account and that of his attorneys, followed every step ORR asked of him.

Reunification nonetheless stalled for months. ORR told the father it could not schedule the fingerprinting appointment required to clear him as a sponsor; after attorneys intervened by letter in February and the agency finally allowed the fingerprinting, home visit, and DNA test, ORR stalled again and offered no timeline for release even after he had been treated as a viable sponsor. The delay tracked a broader policy shift: the Associated Press reported that average ORR custody times rose from 37 days when the administration took office in January 2025 to nearly 200 days by February 2026, as stricter sponsor-vetting rules took effect and attorneys increasingly turned to emergency habeas corpus petitions to force releases. Attorneys filed such a petition for the girl on Feb. 18, 2026; ORR released her to her father two days later, the family reunited Feb. 21, and the case was closed March 5, 2026.

The recordable abuse here is the government's prolonged detention of a young child and its denial of due process in reuniting her with a qualifying parent — a documented government action distinct from the separate allegation, central to the family's lawsuit, that the girl was sexually abused by an older child at the foster placement. That allegation is an accusation against a non-state actor and is treated here as context, not as a mapped abuse. Legal advocates, including the National Center for Youth Law, characterized the case as "yet another version of family separation" and said the administration has repeatedly flouted statutory obligations to release detained children to their families quickly and safely. The family now lives in Chicago with the girl's grandparents while her immigration case proceeds.
