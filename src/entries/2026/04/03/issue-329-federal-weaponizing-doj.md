On April 3, 2026, Chief U.S. District Judge James Boasberg of the District of Columbia denied the Justice Department's motion to reconsider his earlier order quashing two grand-jury subpoenas served on the Federal Reserve's Board of Governors. The subpoenas, issued by D.C. U.S. Attorney Jeanine Pirro, were part of a criminal probe of Fed Chair Jerome Powell tied to cost overruns on the Fed's headquarters renovation and Powell's related congressional testimony. Boasberg wrote that the government's arguments did "not come close to convincing the Court that a different outcome is warranted."

The ruling upheld Boasberg's March 13 finding that there was "a mountain of evidence" the subpoenas were intended to pressure Powell into cutting interest rates or resigning, and "essentially zero evidence" of any underlying crime. The court characterized the subpoenas as pretextual and situated them within a broader "pattern" of politically motivated DOJ investigations of the president's perceived adversaries — language that moves the conduct from allegation toward judicially found fact.

The denial started a 30-day appeal clock, and Pirro said the Justice Department would appeal, characterizing Boasberg's decision as the work of an "activist judge." The episode documents the use of federal prosecutorial process against the head of an independent agency: the central act maps to weaponizing the Justice Department, and the court's explicit pretext finding maps to politicized investigations. This April 3 reconsideration denial is a facet of the same subpoena-weaponization sequence that began with the March 13 quashing.
