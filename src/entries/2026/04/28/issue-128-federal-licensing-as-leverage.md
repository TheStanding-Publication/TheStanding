On April 28, 2026, the Federal Communications Commission directed Disney's ABC to file broadcast license-renewal applications for its eight owned-and-operated television stations within 30 days — by May 28, 2026 — years ahead of their scheduled renewal dates in 2028 and later. The early call-in, authorized under 47 CFR § 73.3539, is essentially without modern precedent for a major broadcaster. The order came days after President Trump and First Lady Melania Trump publicly urged ABC to fire late-night host Jimmy Kimmel over a joke about Melania, which she had called "hateful and violent." The FCC tied its action to an ongoing probe of possible Communications Act violations, including alleged DEI-related discrimination, and to the "public interest" standard.

Press-freedom advocates and ABC characterized the move as unprecedented government retaliation for protected speech. FCC Commissioner Anna Gomez and the Freedom of the Press Foundation warned that accelerating license review of a broadcaster in the wake of White House anger over a comedian's joke could chill protected expression. The central act — the use of the FCC's broadcast-licensing authority to single out one company's stations for early scrutiny, timed to and publicly linked with executive pressure over speech — maps to licensing or regulatory power used as leverage and to the targeting of critics with government power. The agency's stated DEI and Communications Act rationale is noted, but the documented retaliatory framing and the near-total absence of precedent for an early call-in of this kind sit at the core of the event.

ABC subsequently submitted the renewal applications "under protest," calling the FCC's order unlawful, arbitrary, and unconstitutional, and signaling a legal challenge framing the review as an assault on the free press. Those downstream developments are corroborating context; this entry is anchored to the April 28 order itself.
