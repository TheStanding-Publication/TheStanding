The U.S. Department of Defense awarded roughly $24 million in
research contracts to Foundation Future Industries, a San Francisco
humanoid-robotics startup, with awards spread across the U.S. Army,
Navy, and Air Force. The contracts fund testing of the company's
"Phantom" robots — described by the firm and by Fox Business as
designed to "breach enemy sites and spare American lives" — for
military applications. Foundation has already deployed two Phantom
MK-1 units to Ukraine in February 2026 for logistics and
reconnaissance testing. The Pentagon-side procurement record, the
firm's role as the contracting counterparty, and the deployment
trial are reported consistently across Democracy Now!, Fox Business,
and The Next Web.

The Standing's interest is not in the robots but in the structural
relationship: Eric Trump, son of the sitting president, serves as
chief strategy adviser at the recipient firm, and on Thursday,
April 23, 2026, he appeared on Fox Business's "Mornings with
Maria" alongside CEO Sankaet Pathak to promote the deal,
framing it as part of "winning robotics" for the United States.
Democracy Now! reported on the promotion the following morning
under the headline "Eric Trump Touts $24 Million Pentagon Contract
Awarded to His Robotics Company." The president's immediate family
holds a senior advisory role at, and is publicly promoting, a
private firm now drawing $24 million in research contracts from
the federal department the president commands. Massachusetts
Senator Elizabeth Warren publicly characterized the arrangement
as "corruption in plain sight," asking whether the Pentagon is
"just a cash machine for Trump's kids now."

This entry records the pattern under two abuses in The Standing's
taxonomy: officials using public power to enrich close associates
(self-dealing) and using the official position to generate private
revenue for the family (monetizing-office). Adjacent slugs were
considered and set aside. `undisclosed-financial-conflicts` does
not apply because the relationship is publicly disclosed and the
benefitting party is a family member rather than a federal
official. `nepotism` in this taxonomy covers appointments of
family members in violation of anti-nepotism rules; Eric Trump
holds no federal appointment, so the slug does not fit even though
the colloquial sense of nepotism is in play.
`procurement-irregularities` would apply if reporting established
that competitive bidding was bypassed or steered to the firm; no
public reporting on the record as of the archive date makes that
claim, so the slug is omitted. The Standing records the conflict
as it stands on the public record: a $24 million federal contract
to a firm at which the president's son holds a senior strategic
role and is publicly promoting the deal.
