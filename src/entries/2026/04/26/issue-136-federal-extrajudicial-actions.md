On Sunday, April 26, 2026, U.S. Southern Command announced a "lethal
kinetic strike" on a boat in the eastern Pacific Ocean that it said
was carrying drugs along known smuggling routes, killing three people.
SOUTHCOM posted a video on X showing the vessel moving across the
water before an explosion left it in flames, and repeated its standard
statement that it had targeted alleged drug traffickers. The military
provided no public evidence that the boat, or any vessel hit in the
campaign, was carrying drugs, and those aboard were afforded none of
the due-process protections normally extended to criminal suspects.
Asked about an earlier eastern-Pacific strike that killed two men, a
SOUTHCOM spokesperson told CBS News only that "for operational
security reasons, we cannot discuss specific sources or methods."

The strike was one of at least eight boat strikes in the Caribbean and
eastern Pacific during April 2026 alone, part of an open-ended
military campaign against alleged drug-trafficking vessels in Latin
American waters that began on September 2, 2025. By April 26 the
campaign's cumulative reported death toll had reached at least 186
people. President Trump has said the United States is in an "armed
conflict" with Latin American cartels, several of which his
administration has designated as terrorist organizations, and has
justified the strikes as a necessary escalation against the flow of
drugs. Congress has not authorized hostilities against those
organizations, and military lawyers, legal scholars, and Democratic
lawmakers have argued the strikes amount to extrajudicial killings of
criminal suspects outside any congressionally authorized armed
conflict.

This April 26 eastern-Pacific strike is recorded as its own event
under The Standing's event-level deduplication rule: it differs in
date from the archived April 19, 2026 Caribbean strike
