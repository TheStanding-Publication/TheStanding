On June 2, 2026, President Donald Trump named Bill Pulte — director of the Federal Housing Finance Agency and chairman of Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac — as acting Director of National Intelligence. Pulte succeeds Tulsi Gabbard, who announced in May that she would resign effective June 30. Pulte, a mortgage-finance executive with no prior intelligence or national-security experience, will retain his FHFA role while serving atop the intelligence community. Under the Federal Vacancies Reform Act, the acting designation can run until January 26, 2027.

Critics across both parties said the appointment circumvents the statutory requirement, codified at 50 U.S.C. § 3023, that any individual appointed Director of National Intelligence "shall have extensive national security expertise." Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer called Pulte "a partisan thug with no experience in intelligence," and several Republican senators questioned his credentials. The concern voiced by lawmakers is that the administration is installing a political loyalist over the intelligence community to shape findings to the White House's preferences; in his FHFA role Pulte had sent criminal referrals to the Justice Department alleging mortgage fraud by Trump political adversaries, including New York Attorney General Letitia James, Sen. Adam Schiff, Federal Reserve Governor Lisa Cook, and former Rep. Eric Swalwell.

Whether the "extensive national security expertise" qualification binds an *acting* designation made under the Vacancies Reform Act is legally contestable, so the statutory-requirements mapping reflects the editorial concern raised by members of Congress rather than a settled legal conclusion. This entry records the appointment itself; any subsequent action by Pulte to suppress or distort intelligence products would be a separate, later event. The appointment follows earlier reporting on Gabbard's tenure, including her criminal referrals targeting an impeachment whistleblower.
