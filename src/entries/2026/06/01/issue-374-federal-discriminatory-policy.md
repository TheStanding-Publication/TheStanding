In a 2-1 decision issued June 1, 2026, a panel of the U.S. Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit allowed the Trump administration, for now, to continue barring transgender people from enlisting in the military, while leaving in place a block on the expulsion of transgender troops already serving. Writing for the majority, Judge Robert Wilkins found that the administration's 2025 policy appeared to be driven "by the bare desire to harm a politically unpopular group: persons who identify as transgender." The court nonetheless held that the Pentagon's broad statutory authority to set enlistment standards permits the entry ban to remain in force while the underlying lawsuit proceeds.

The policy originates in a January 2025 executive order in which President Donald Trump asserted that adopting a transgender identity "conflicts with a soldier's commitment to an honorable, truthful, and disciplined lifestyle." Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth issued the implementing directive, which presumptively disqualifies people with gender dysphoria from service. The ruling partially upholds a 2025 decision by U.S. District Judge Ana Reyes, who had blocked the policy entirely as likely unconstitutional sex discrimination; the Supreme Court allowed the policy to take effect in May 2025 without explaining its reasoning.

The panel split on remedy: the court affirmed the injunction protecting current service members from removal but vacated it as to would-be enlistees, with Trump appointee Judge Justin Walker joining to lift the bar on enforcement against new recruits. The adjudicating court is not itself an actor in the abuse documented here — the discriminatory policy is the executive branch's military-service ban. Estimates of the number of transgender service members affected range from the low thousands, per Defense Department figures, to roughly 15,000 by advocates' accounts.
