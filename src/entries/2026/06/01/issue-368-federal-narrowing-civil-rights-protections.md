The U.S. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission is moving to dismantle two of
the federal government's oldest tools for detecting and remedying workplace
discrimination. Under chair Andrea Lucas, a Trump appointee, the agency has
submitted to the White House Office of Information and Regulatory Affairs a
proposal to rescind a 1979 interpretive rule that has, for more than four
decades, set out the circumstances under which private employers may adopt
voluntary affirmative-action plans under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of
1964. That rule encouraged employers to analyze their own workforces for
adverse impact on protected groups and to take reasonable, voluntary steps —
including goals and timetables — to correct imbalances it revealed. Separately,
the EEOC is pursuing an end to the annual EEO-1 report, which requires employers
with 100 or more workers to disclose the race, ethnicity, sex, and job-category
breakdown of their workforce.

The EEO-1 data has historically been the federal government's primary lens for
seeing where women and people of color are not being hired or promoted, and the
analysis it enables has underpinned discrimination settlements worth billions of
dollars since the 1960s. Ending the collection would not withhold data already
gathered so much as stop the government from gathering it at all, removing the
empirical basis on which disparate-impact cases are built. Rescinding the 1979
rule would strip the agency's formal blessing from the voluntary remedial
measures employers have long relied on to stay ahead of liability. The moves are
consistent with the Trump administration's broader characterization of diversity,
equity, and inclusion efforts as themselves discriminatory.

These are deregulatory agency actions advanced through the administrative
process rather than a single dated order, so this entry is dated to the June 1,
2026 reporting; the specific notice-of-proposed-rulemaking and rescission dates
will firm up as the actions clear OIRA review. Rescinding the 1979 voluntary
affirmative-action regulation maps to `narrowing-civil-rights-protections` — an
agency materially weakening an existing protection short of a formal statutory
repeal. Ending EEO-1 collection is mapped to `suppression-of-government-data`,
with the nuance that it eliminates the future collection of demographic data
rather than withholding data already held, which is the closest applicable
characterization. And `dismantling-agency-capacity` captures the hollowing of a
statutorily grounded enforcement function under Title VII through agency action
taken outside the legislative process. The actions fit a wider pattern of
civil-rights rollbacks recorded in the archive.
