José Guadalupe Ramos-Solano, a 52-year-old Mexican national, was found
unconscious and unresponsive in his bunk at the Adelanto ICE Processing Center
in San Bernardino County, California, at approximately 9:29 p.m. on March 25,
2026. Security staff moved him to Victor Valley Global Medical Center in
Victorville, where he was pronounced dead. U.S. Immigration and Customs
Enforcement had arrested him on February 23, 2026 during a targeted operation
in Torrance and detained him at Adelanto, a facility operated for ICE by the
private prison company The GEO Group. ICE said a February 24 health evaluation
identified diabetes, hyperlipidemia, and hypertension, for which it stated he
was being treated.

His death was at least the fourteenth recorded in ICE custody in 2026.
Representatives Judy Chu and Ted Lieu publicly demanded answers, questioning
whether inadequate medical care or systemic failures contributed to the death,
and the Mexican government — including President Claudia Sheinbaum and the
consulates in Los Angeles and San Bernardino — described the death as part of
an "alarming and unacceptable trend" while signaling support for litigation
over detention conditions and a referral to the Inter-American Commission on
Human Rights. Other detainees alleged that they tried to alert staff to
Ramos-Solano's deteriorating condition — symptoms of overheating and difficulty
breathing — but that he was not attended to until he was already unresponsive,
and some asserted he died in his bunk rather than at the hospital. ICE's account
and the detainees' accounts diverge on these points, and that divergence is part
of the public record of the event.

ICE did not immediately release an official cause of death. Subsequent
investigative reporting on an autopsy attributed the death to COVID-19 contracted
while in custody, with high blood pressure and cardiovascular disease listed as
contributing causes. The death of a person held in federal immigration detention
is the paradigm `deaths-in-custody` event: the state's coercive power over a
detained person carries a duty of care whose breach is measured by oversight and
consequence, and an in-custody death is the sharpest test of that accountability.
This entry records Ramos-Solano's death specifically; the broader 2026 toll of
ICE-custody deaths is context rather than a separate archivable act.
