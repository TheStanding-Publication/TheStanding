On March 31, 2026, U.S. District Judge Allison Burroughs of the District
of Massachusetts ruled that the Department of Homeland Security acted
unlawfully when it terminated the humanitarian parole of more than 900,000
people who had entered the United States lawfully through the Biden-era
CBP One application. The court vacated the terminations and ordered federal
officials to restore parole status for the affected class. Many of those
parolees had been granted two-year stays and work authorization after
scheduling appointments at ports of entry through the app.

The underlying action began in April 2025, when DHS sent mass, unsigned
emails informing parole recipients that their status had been revoked and
that they were expected to leave the country "immediately" — within seven
days — or face removal. The notices were not individualized: the agency
did not show that a qualified official had reviewed each case and
determined that the purpose of each person's parole had been fulfilled, a
step required under federal law and the agency's own regulations. Burroughs
found that the terminations "exceeded the agency's statutory authority and
contradicted the procedures set forth in its own regulations." By the time
of the ruling, an unknown number of affected migrants had already been
deported.

The episode is recorded here as the executive branch's en-masse
termination of a lawful immigration status without individualized notice
or hearing — a denial of due process in immigration enforcement, a denial
of hearing for stripping a legal status from an entire class without one,
and a disregard of the statutory and regulatory procedures governing
parole. The court ruling is the corroborating record that establishes the
action's unlawful character; Judge Burroughs and the plaintiffs (Democracy
Forward, the Massachusetts Law Reform Institute, and the Venezuelan
Association of Massachusetts, representing Venezuelan, Cuban, and Haitian
parolees) are the corrective and target parties, not actors of the abuse.
A DHS spokesperson disputed the decision, calling it "blatant judicial
activism."
