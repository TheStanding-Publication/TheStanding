On March 31, 2026, U.S. District Judge Richard Leon of the District of
Columbia issued a 35-page opinion granting a preliminary injunction that
halted further above-ground construction of President Trump's roughly
$400 million ballroom, a structure of about 89,000 square feet planned for
the former site of the demolished White House East Wing. The lawsuit was
brought by the National Trust for Historic Preservation, which argued that
the executive branch had begun a major demolition-and-construction project
on a federal landmark without the congressional authorization federal law
requires. Leon agreed, writing that the President "is not ... the owner" of
the White House and that the statute permitting the "care, maintenance,
repair," and "alteration" of the building does not stretch to "the
wholesale demolition of entire buildings and construction of new ones."

The archivable conduct is the executive branch proceeding with that
project absent statutory authorization — a separation-of-powers question
on which the court explicitly identified Congress as the proper authorizing
body. Leon's order made the boundary plain: "unless and until Congress
blesses this project through statutory authorization, construction has to
stop!" The court ruling is the documentary record confirming the overreach
was an actual, ongoing course of conduct — construction was already
underway, not merely proposed — which clears the actually-happening
threshold for the archive. The judge and the plaintiff are contextual
parties to the dispute, not actors of the abuse; the actors are the
President and the Executive Office of the President that advanced the
project.

The administration said it would appeal, and Leon delayed implementation
of his order for two weeks to allow it to do so. In subsequent proceedings
the administration argued that a safety-and-security exception in the
order permitted the work to continue; Leon rejected the broad reading,
holding that "national security is not a blank check to proceed with
otherwise unlawful activity," while allowing narrowly necessary security
work to go forward. Related facets of the ballroom matter — reported DOJ
pressure on the National Trust to drop the suit (issue #137) and a no-bid
White House grounds contract (issue #92) — are tracked as distinct events
and may warrant grouping with this entry as an episode, an editorial call
left to episode discovery.
