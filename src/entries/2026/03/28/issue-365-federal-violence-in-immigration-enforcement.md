On the night of Saturday, March 28, 2026, hundreds of people gathered outside
the U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement facility in South Portland after
the day's third nationwide "No Kings" demonstrations. The facility's front gate
was broken around 7 p.m. and, after federal agents temporarily repaired it,
broken a second time. Federal agents posted atop the building were observed
firing pepper balls — kinetic crowd-control munitions — into the crowd around
the time the Portland Police Bureau declared the gathering an unlawful assembly,
just after 10 p.m. Police warned that those who did not clear the road in front
of the facility could be subject to crowd-control measures, then made targeted
arrests; three people were arrested outside the facility.

The federal agents' use of pepper-ball munitions against a crowd in the course
of an immigration-enforcement posture is recorded here as
`violence-in-immigration-enforcement`, with `excessive-force-by-law-enforcement`
capturing the proportionality dimension of force deployed from the facility
against assembled demonstrators. Reporting noted that agents wore gas masks
during the evening but that no tear gas was used; the munitions confirmed in the
crowd were pepper balls.

This deployment sits against an active legal record over crowd-control weapons
at the same building. A federal judge had previously restricted agents' use of
tear gas and munitions against crowds at the South Portland facility, and a
federal appeals court reversed that limitation shortly before this protest,
restoring broader latitude to use crowd-control weapons there. That context
sharpens the accountability stakes of this specific deployment. A separate
incident at the same facility on May 1, 2026 — federal agents firing pepper
balls into a May Day crowd — is a distinct event recorded under event-level
deduplication, cross-referenced here as a pattern at the same location rather
