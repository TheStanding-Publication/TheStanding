In late March 2026, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth made the highly unusual move
of intervening in the regular military promotion process to strike four Army
officers — two Black men and two women — from the list of nominees for
promotion to brigadier general, the Army's one-star rank. The officers had been
selected on merit by a promotion board, and Hegseth removed them over the
objections of Army Secretary Daniel Driscoll, who said the officers had
exemplary records and had done nothing wrong. According to reporting first
published by The New York Times and confirmed by NPR through U.S. officials,
one Black officer was singled out over a paper written years earlier examining
why Black officers historically chose support roles over front-line combat
positions, and one of the women was targeted in connection with her service
during the 2021 U.S. withdrawal from Afghanistan. NPR further reported that a
Black colonel and a female colonel from another branch were also removed,
bringing the total to at least six blocked promotions.

The removals fit within a broader pattern under Hegseth, who before his
appointment wrote books disparaging diversity in the ranks and who has since
fired Joint Chiefs Chairman Gen. CQ Brown — the second African American to hold
the post — and Adm. Lisa Franchetti, the first woman to lead the Navy, in both
cases without stated cause. Pentagon rules permit the secretary to pull a name
from a promotion list only for moral, mental, physical, or professional
failings; neither the Defense Department nor the White House offered any
performance-based explanation for these removals. Pentagon spokesman Sean
Parnell called the reporting "fake news," stating that under Hegseth
"military promotions are given to those who have earned them" and that
meritocracy in the department is "apolitical and unbiased."

Removing named officers from a merit-based promotion list on the basis of race
and sex is recorded here as `discriminatory-policy`: the protection of equal
treatment under law did not extend to these officers, and identity was the
decisive factor. Overriding the uniformed chain of command for ideological
("anti-DEI") reasons, against the judgment of the Army's own civilian
secretary, adds `politicization-of-uniformed-services` as a secondary abuse.
Sen. Jack Reed, ranking member of the Senate Armed Services Committee, said
that removing four decorated officers selected by their peers for merit would
be "not only outrageous" but "illegal." The Pentagon's dispute over the
characterization is recorded as a caveat; the underlying personnel action is
corroborated across multiple independent outlets. This event is distinct from
the broader Hegseth officer-purge cluster and the prior firings of Gen. Brown
and Adm. Franchetti, which stand as pattern context rather than part of this
record.
