On March 30, 2026, nearly a dozen U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement agents surrounded Salah Sarsour's car shortly after he left his home in the Milwaukee area and took him into custody. Sarsour, 53, is a Palestinian-born lawful permanent resident who has lived in the United States for more than 30 years and has served for five years as board president of the Islamic Society of Milwaukee, the largest Islamic organization in Wisconsin. His attorneys say he holds a green card; his wife and four adult children are U.S. citizens, and he has no criminal record in the United States.

His attorneys say the government justified the detention on the grounds that Sarsour is a "foreign policy threat," a designation they call meritless and have likened to the case of former Columbia University activist Mahmoud Khalil. The Department of Homeland Security has separately pointed to a conviction in an Israeli military court — incurred when Sarsour was a minor in the Israeli-occupied West Bank, for offenses his attorney describes as throwing rocks at Israeli officers and which DHS has characterized as throwing Molotov cocktails — and to alleged misstatements on his immigration paperwork. His lawyers note the U.S. government has known of the Israeli conviction since he arrived in 1993, and supporters argue the arrest is political retaliation for his advocacy on behalf of Palestinians.

Sarsour has been held at a county jail in Indiana, an ICE-contracting facility, while removal proceedings move forward; his attorneys have filed a petition seeking his release. The recorded abuse is the detention itself: the sudden, heavily resourced arrest of a long-tenured lawful permanent resident with deep U.S. ties on a foreign-policy-threat designation, followed by out-of-state confinement and removal proceedings that bypass the protections ordinarily afforded such a resident — squarely a denial of due process in immigration enforcement and unlawful detention. The speech-and-assembly and discriminatory-targeting dimensions raised by supporters are noted but not mapped as abuses here, because their abusive character currently rests on the supporters' account rather than the government's own established action.
