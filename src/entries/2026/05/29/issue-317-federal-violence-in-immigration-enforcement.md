On May 29, 2026, four people detained at Camp East Montana filed a federal
class-action complaint against U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement in the
U.S. District Court for the Western District of Texas, the first lawsuit
targeting what is now the largest immigration detention facility in the
country. The facility is a tent camp erected in the Chihuahuan Desert on the
U.S. Army's Fort Bliss base in El Paso, opened in August 2025 and built toward
a 5,000-bed capacity, holding a daily average of more than 2,500 detainees as
of early April. Brought by the ACLU, the ACLU of Texas, the Texas Civil Rights
Project, Human Rights Watch, and the law firm Farella Braun + Martel, the suit
seeks class certification on behalf of all those detained at the camp and asks
the court to find that conditions there violate detainees' Fifth Amendment
right to due process and the Administrative Procedure Act.

The complaint describes a pattern of abuse: violent uses of force by guards,
coercive threats of deportation, excessive and arbitrary solitary confinement,
severe medical neglect, rancid and inadequate food, and dangerous, unsanitary
living conditions. One named plaintiff, Gerald Akari Angye, says guards beat
him so severely he was hospitalized and left needing a wrist brace, then held
him in solitary confinement for 15 days. The abusive character of the camp does
not rest on the plaintiffs' allegations alone: in under a year of operation the
facility has recorded at least three detainee deaths — one of them ruled a
homicide, a man reportedly beaten to death by guards after asking for his
inhaler — a roughly monthlong measles outbreak, and nearly 50 detention-
standards violations identified by ICE's own Office of Detention Oversight
inspectors. Members of Congress, including Rep. Veronica Escobar, have
corroborated conditions through oversight visits and pressed for the camp's
closure.

The Department of Homeland Security called the claims "categorically false,"
saying ICE prioritizes the safety and well-being of those in its custody and
that no detainees are beaten or abused. ICE replaced the facility's prime
contractor in March 2026, but advocates said in a May 22 letter that conditions
"continued to deteriorate." Because the suit was filed only days before this
entry was recorded, the litigation is at its earliest stage; the entry records
the documented conditions and the filing itself, not any judicial finding, and
may warrant review as the case proceeds. The named institutional defendant is
ICE; the monitoring issue's reference to individually named defendants (an ICE
director and the DHS Secretary) could not be confirmed against the filed
complaint this run and is left out pending verification.
