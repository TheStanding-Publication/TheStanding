On May 11, 2026, *The New York Times* reported that internal
Department of Justice case-tracking documents it had obtained showed
the Board of Immigration Appeals — the appellate tribunal that hears
immigration appeals from within the Department of Justice — moved
Palestinian activist Mahmoud Khalil's deportation case along an
unusually fast procedural track. According to the documents, Khalil's
case was flagged as high-priority before the board officially
received the file; an internal staff note instructed BIA personnel
to handle the case as if Khalil were still in detention — a status
that accelerates adjudication — even though he had already been
released; the board's chair was personally involved in the case from
its early stages; and the BIA's April 9, 2026 ruling that Khalil
could be deported came just nine days after the paperwork was
submitted, in a category of case the board often takes years to
decide. At least three judges recused themselves from the
proceedings.

Homero López, a former BIA member appointed under President Biden
and later fired under President Trump, told the *Times* the timeline
was "unprecedented" and "an insane turnaround, particularly for such
a high-profile case on a novel legal issue." Responding to the
reporting on social media, Khalil characterized the procedure as
"corrupt and unprecedented" and said the administration had "put me
through a sham immigration process while guaranteeing the outcome in
advance." Khalil's legal team — the Center for Constitutional
Rights, ACLU, and NYCLU — filed a motion the same day asking the
BIA to reopen Khalil's case and terminate proceedings, citing the
documented procedural irregularities as evidence the original
decision was tainted.

The BIA is statutorily required to make independent decisions, but
sits administratively inside the Department of Justice and is staffed
by attorneys whose appointments and removals run through the Attorney
General's office. The documents disclosed by the *Times* place the
Khalil ruling squarely inside that institutional friction: a high-
profile case adjudicated against a politically prominent activist
target, with the appellate process compressed to a fraction of its
ordinary timeline and with the administration's preferred outcome
locked in before independent review could meaningfully occur.
