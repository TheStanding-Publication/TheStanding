A pattern emerging across federal courts shows that U.S. Immigration and
Customs Enforcement has held immigrants in detention for months at a time by
asserting that another country would soon agree to receive them. Many of
those detained had already won orders from immigration courts withholding
their removal because they faced persecution or torture in their home
countries. According to a review by Documented NY, federal habeas judges
repeatedly found ICE's third-country assertions to be unsupported: since
January 1, 2026, courts have issued at least 167 orders freeing such
detainees on the ground that the agency had no reasonable prospect of
arranging a third-country placement. In a smaller set of roughly 35 cases the
government prevailed, mostly because judges concluded the habeas petitions had
been filed prematurely rather than because the detentions were justified.

The releases build on a separate, earlier ruling. In February 2026, U.S.
District Judge Brian Murphy of Massachusetts held that the administration's
policy of removing people with final orders to third countries -- without
notice or a meaningful opportunity to object -- violates federal immigration
law and the constitutional guarantee of due process, and ordered the
government to provide advance notice before any such removal. The volume of
subsequent habeas releases indicates the underlying detention practice
continued in the face of that ruling, and reporting indicates ICE has in some
instances re-detained individuals on the same third-country theory after
courts ordered their release, fueling continued litigation.

The Standing records this as a systemic pattern rather than a single case:
unlawful detention of people who hold protective immigration-court orders,
a denial of due process in immigration enforcement, the repeated need for
habeas courts to compel release, and conduct that -- through re-detention after
release orders -- edges from aggressive legal interpretation toward defiance of
court orders. The scale, at least 167 court-ordered releases in roughly five
months, is what brings the practice into archive scope; it is the volume and
repetition, not any one habeas case, that establishes a default agency
approach rather than an outlier. Appellate review of Judge Murphy's ruling and
the trajectory of the ongoing habeas litigation are the natural next
developments to watch.
