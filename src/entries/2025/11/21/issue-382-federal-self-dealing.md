In November 2025, the Pentagon's Office of Strategic Capital (OSC) committed a roughly $620 million conditional loan to Vulcan Elements, a rare-earth magnet startup based in Durham, North Carolina — the largest loan in the office's history — as part of a joint commitment with ReElement Technologies that the Department of Defense valued at about $700 million. The Department of Commerce separately took a $50 million equity stake in the company. The award went out without competitive bidding and without the independent technical-viability review ordinarily required of OSC borrowers, a review waived under Executive Order 14241. According to the company and the government, the funding was intended to expand domestic production of rare-earth magnets and reduce U.S. dependence on China.

The deal drew scrutiny because of its timing and its beneficiaries. Roughly three months before the November announcement, 1789 Capital — the venture firm where Donald Trump Jr. is a named partner — had taken an undisclosed equity stake in Vulcan as part of a fundraising round. A May 28, 2026 investigation by ProPublica and NPR reported that White House senior counselor Peter Navarro, described as a friend of Trump Jr.'s, personally pressed the Pentagon to issue the loan at unusual speed; of the dozens of companies the Pentagon was weighing for funding at the time, Vulcan's was the only deal initiated by a top White House aide, and it was completed in weeks rather than the usual months of vetting. The president's son said through a spokesperson that he was not involved, and the Pentagon and the company denied any political favoritism.

The exercise of federal funding authority to benefit a firm in which the President's son's venture fund held an undisclosed stake maps to self-dealing, while the record-setting loan's issuance without competitive bidding and without the standard independent technical review maps to procurement irregularities. A March 25, 2026 effort by House Natural Resources Committee Democrats to subpoena Trump Jr. over the deal was blocked by the committee's Republican majority; that vote is corroborating oversight context rather than a separate archivable abuse.
