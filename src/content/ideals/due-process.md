Before the state takes a person's liberty, property, or status, there must be notice, an adequate hearing, counsel where required, and a meaningful opportunity to be heard. These procedural protections are old, and they exist because the alternative — the state acting unilaterally against individuals — has consistently produced injustice everywhere it has been tried. Due process is what makes the difference between a lawful state and an arbitrary one.

The abuses tracked under this ideal include the [denial of counsel](/ideals/due-process/denial-of-counsel/) where it is required, [detention without lawful authority or beyond authorized duration](/ideals/due-process/unlawful-detention/), [deprivation of liberty without an adequate hearing](/ideals/due-process/denial-of-hearing/), the [refusal to honor habeas petitions](/ideals/due-process/ignoring-habeas/), the [bypassing of required procedural protections in immigration enforcement](/ideals/due-process/denial-of-due-process-in-immigration-enforcement/), and [extrajudicial state action](/ideals/due-process/extrajudicial-actions/) that punishes without adjudication. The principle applies to citizens and non-citizens alike, in immigration courts as in criminal ones, because the constitutional question is about what the government may do, not about who the target is.
